The front office and risk models can look at the same instrument  and come up with different P&L directions.
For example: a current corporate bond front office model doesn't actually model whether a company is getting riskier. 
At best, it relies on the actual bond price and works backwards to figure out what it implies about that company's risk. ( implied spread) 

The risk model works differently :  it skips individual prices entirely and credit sprad and instead groups companies into broad rating buckets
assuming all names in the same bucket move together and carry the same risk. 

But when something company-specific happens — like SVB collapsing and regional banks getting stressed,SVB and JP Morgan would get  treated as if they carry the same risk.
 When one name blows up and the rest are fine, the risk model can't tell the difference. and the front office model would catches it only  through the price. 

This isn't just a one-off issue with banks. It applies to any product where we rely on market price to impply a spread — risky bonds, bond futures, listed futures.
